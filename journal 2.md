Journal of Jean-François DuPlessis

A Jasadin Testament recovered from the Farmhouse Door


---

The First Entry

I am Jean‑François DuPlessis, farmer of the Evangeline Valley along the St. Jimothy River.  I set these words down so that whoever finds them may know my folly and steer clear of the same trespass.  I believe I have broken the Order of the Ruby Matron, Our Lady of Flame and Bone.  I beg that She forgive me and guide my soul to Her Silent Library when my time comes.

It began near twenty years ago, when I found a door standing upright in the woods—no frame, no hinges, just standing like it belonged there.  The wood was smooth, carved all of one piece, the grain tight as if polished by centuries.  The knob was metal I never saw before, cold and red‑hued, turning smoother than any smith in the valley could manage.

I felt a shiver run through me then.  I should have left it for the priestess to judge, but I thought it a fine door, and a door is just a door to a farmer.  I whispered a small prayer to the Ruby Matron to keep mischance away and took it home.

The thing was light to carry yet refused to turn over in my hands, like it wished to keep its front toward me.  I thought little of it.  Fool that I was.

For two years it sat behind the hay in my barn, forgotten.  Then, when our front door rotted off its hinges, I remembered the red‑metal door.  It fit perfect, too perfect—as if the house had been built for it.  Hinges showed where none had been, and the latch caught clean on a plate that appeared between my glances.  I told my wife it was Providence; she praised my handiwork.  I see now it was the Lady's silence—Her way of waiting for me to notice the wrong.

After that, all seemed well for near a year.  But one evening the door no longer opened to the valley.  My wife and the babe were gone, and beyond the threshold lay a strange field under an empty sky.  Trees I never saw grew there, their bark like bone.  I turned to look back and saw only the door standing alone, my house gone behind me.  That was the first time I knew the Matron had turned Her gaze away.

I prayed to Her, poured blessed ashwater on the frame, and begged to be set right in Her ledger.  The water only wet the wood.  I prayed until my knees gave out.  No answer came.


---

The Second Entry

I took my axe to the door but the blade glanced like I struck stone.  Windows showed nothing of the valley now—just a light that pulsed, yellow and wrong.  I feared to sleep, fearing to wake in some other place.

In the cellar I heard movement beyond the storm doors.  I thought it a coyote until I saw its shadow stretch like a man.  I fetched the axe and waited.  The latch lifted by unseen hand and in came a beast shaped of rat and man both, teeth square as chisels, eyes bright as coals.  I struck and by the Matron’s mercy split its skull.  I burned the carcass and buried the ashes with two crosses of redwood, one beneath and one above, whispering the Rite of Passage as best I knew it.

I should have called for a priestess then, though none could find this place now.  I am certain the door unmoored the house from the world.  The Lady’s laws are for the living; this place sits between.


---

The Third Entry

Food from the woods does not rot.  Water stays clear though weeks have passed.  I thought it blessing at first, but I know now it is stasis—the Matron’s quill frozen above my name, unable to write the end of me.  I am trapped outside the ledger.

The forest holds beasts not of any world I knew: a hairless moose with a bull’s horns, and behind it a scaled bear tall as my roof.  When it came for me I fled through the door, praying aloud.  The latch shut of its own, and silence fell so hard my ears rang.  When I opened the door again, the world beyond had changed—a vast chamber of wood‑panelled walls and ceilings too high to see.  My house stands in a field inside some grand hall that shifts each time I venture out.


---

The Fourth Entry

These halls are endless.  I believe this is what the priestesses call the Unfiled Margin—the place of souls not yet delivered.  The corridors breathe, the air hums like a hive.  I hear whispers that echo long after I speak.

I have set rules for myself, and for any poor soul who reads this:

1. Keep stillness.  The halls listen for motion.


2. Watch the collectors.  Little metal spiders scuttle about, sorting debris.  Follow them and you may find food or tools.  Cross them and they will try to sort you.


3. Touch not the nodules.  Bulging sacs in the walls.  Some hide gifts, others death.  Break one only if hunger forces you.


4. Never speak your true name aloud.  Echoes learn it and use it against you.


5. Never tell another soul where the house lies.  It is the only refuge I have.


6. If a droning fills the air, return at once and bar the door.  Stay out of the light.


7. If you hear wings above, do not look up.


8. When you return, search every room.  Things come in uninvited.


9. Pray before and after each venture.  The Lady notes persistence.



I live by these laws now, hoping they keep me within Her notice.


---

The Fifth Entry

I have found others’ traces—candles burned low, prints in the dust—but never the folk themselves.  I fear they were taken to fill the empty lines in the Matron’s book.

Today a voice called my name.  I turned and saw myself, eyeless, smiling wide.  It said I was in its house.  I ran for the cellar and barred the storm doors.  It beat upon them, begging and cursing by turns.  I clutched my tally beads and prayed every verse I knew.  At the final bead the pounding ceased.

The candles gutter low.  I feel Her presence faint but steady, like the ember in a spent log.  Perhaps She still watches, waiting for me to finish my confession.


---

The Last Entry (half a page, torn)

It has been a year, I think, though no sun marks the time.  I found this book among the halls, mostly blank, and set my tale to it.  My memory frays; I forget the sound of wind, the smell of rain.  Only the ticking of the halls keeps me company.

If you find this, know that the Ruby Matron did not make this place.  It is an error—an unbalanced entry, a page gone missing from Her great Ledger.  Do not stay long.  Do not trust the door.

Hold Her in your heart and speak plain when you die, so She may write you true.

May the Ruby Matron receive me at last, and forgive the farmer who thought a door was just a door.
