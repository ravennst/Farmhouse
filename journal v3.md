I am Jean-Francois DuPlessis, a farmer of the Evangeline Valley along the Sanctar Velencia River. I set these words down so that whoever finds them may know my follow and steer clear of the same trespass. I believe I have committed blasphemies before the Ruby Matron, Our Lady of Flame and Bone. I beg that She forgive me and guide my sould to Her Silent Library when my time comes.

It began near twenty years ago, when I found a door standing upright in the woods. No frame, no hinges, just standing there like it had always belonged. The wood was black and smooth, carved all of one piece, the grain tight as if plished by centuries. The knob was metal I had never beholden before, cold and red-hued, turning smoother than any smith in the valley could manage.

I felt a shiver run through me then. I should have left it for the Matron Priestess to judge, but I thought it a fine door, and a door is just a door to a farmer. I whispered a small prayer to Our Lady to keep mischance away and took it home. The door was light to carry yet refused to any effort to turn it from an upright position. That should have been my second warning.

For two years it sat behind the hay in my barn, forgotten, until my own front door rotted off its hinges. I thought it odd that it seemed to degrade so quickly but I thought nothing of it. My third warning, ignored. 

I remembered the cursed door in the barn and fetched it to see if it fit. Placing it in the frame it fit, too-perfect, as if the house had been made for it. Hinges showed where none had been and the latch caught clean on a plate that appeared between glances. I praised Our Lady for this divine miracle, I told my wife it was Providence. She thought otherwise. I see it now, if Our Lady of Flame and Bone had any hand in this, it was to test my faith and I fear I failed.

My wife, Therese, left to speak with the Matron Priestess about it. Regardless of my folly, I am grateful to the Goddess that she took little Louis with her for what happened next has been my nightmare ever since. She left for Priestess Magdalaina and swung the door shut behind her. She swung it hard. I expected a slam, I heard a gentle click that resonated through the core of my being. The silence afterwards was deafening. I heard nothing except my own breathing. No birds chirped, the wind shifted outside somehow. The windows clouded over a shade of grey. 

I opened the cursed door after Therese but found a dense forest with the tallest trees I had ever seen. Nothing was familiar. The trees themselves were taller than any church spire, so perfectly straight with needles like porcupine quills. The night sky bore stars in unfamiliar patterns. I did not gaze upon our moon but on two, one whose face bore a pattern of a strange symbol I did not recognize. The smaller one appeared to spin, showing me a different face every time I looked.

I knew I had strayed from the path of Our Ruby Matron and had be cast to damnation, to live out my damnation in ignorance. I stood before the lone door once more, standing upright in the strange wood I found myself in. It floated above the moss a foot, just the door. No frame, no porch, no house. But strangely, I could see inside the farmhouse within. Eventually I gave up trying to will the door to open back where it should. I had gathered what tools I could and attempted to rebuild my life in the strange wood, attempting to build a house by a large lake I found while exploring. I had hoped to redeem myself in the eyes of my Goddess.

The trees proved difficult to chop but I managed and had finished building the walls before I saw them. It had been a solid month since I had found myself in the strange land before I saw them. There were creatures the likes of which I had only imagined but I did not expect to see ones so large. A hairless moose with bulls horns crashed through the trees at the far end of the lake. Even from the distance I was I could tell it was larger than even the farmhouse. It knocked over the giant trees as it ran from what appeared to be an even larger scaled bear. It chased the moose demon towards my new cabin, knocking even more trees over as it chased. Its claws ripped apart the giant trunks like paper.

Taking the axe and musket with me, I ran to the only place I knew to hide, the cursed farmhouse. The prison of my own making. The impossibly large creatures seemed to chase me as I ran, perhaps guided by providence for my damnation. The door seemed to open on its own before me, possibly sensing my urgency. Trees fell like thunder behind me as the demons drew close. I slammed the door shut once again behind me. Again, I did not hear a slam but the gentle click of the latch and then... nothingness. I once again sensed an unsettling shift in the world. The silence burned my ears. 

I sat on my parlor bench and waited. The only noise was the methodic chunking of the wind-up clock in the parlor and the beating of my heart from running in fear. After I regained my composure, I braved myself to open the cursed door once more, only it held fast. Nothing I did could budge it. The rest of the house appeared as I had left it. Even the food in the kitchen had remained, undisturbed... unspoiled.

I resigned myself to my fate and lit a candle for Our Lady of Flame and Bone and once again slept in my own bed. 

I was eating the biscuits my wife had made before this nightmare began, still sitting on the platter on the kitchen counter she had placed over a month ago. They were cold but still soft. I wept in grief.

I do not know how long I had been miserably sitting at the dining table when I heard the sound of something in the cellar. I did not know what it was then, only that it was large like a man but definitely was not one. I had quietly loaded the musket and began to open the door behind the stairs to the cellar. I gave thanks to Our Ruby Matron for the soundless hinges in that moment and waited to see what manner of demon scuttled below me. I did not see it then but heard it creak up the stairs out the side of the cellar through the storm doors going outside.

I crept down with the musket drawn as quietly as I could muster down my own set of stairs. Despite my efforts, they did creak nearly as loud my the beat of mine heart but I made it down without sighting the creature. I did see where it had opened my barrels of tubers and torn open a bag of flour, dragging a trail around the dirt and out the storm doors. Prints of three toes and a fourth like a thumb.
